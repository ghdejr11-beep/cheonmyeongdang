## Couple #1 — The "Officer Meets Output" Pair

**Anonymized Profile:** *Long-distance couple, 4 years together, considering marriage. He's Yang Wood Day Master with strong Direct Officer star. She's Yin Water Day Master with dominant Hurting Officer (상관) star.*

This is one of the most common couple structures we see, and one of the most predictably turbulent.

- **Direct Officer in him** = strong respect for rules, hierarchy, schedules, "the right way to do things"
- **Hurting Officer in her** = creative output that explicitly *defies* rules, rewrites norms, hates being told the proper way

The chart pre-diagnoses the recurring fight: he experiences her as "deliberately disrespectful," she experiences him as "rigid and controlling." Neither is wrong. Both are reading the other through their own dominant Ten Gods.

The Saju recommendation isn't "break up." It's **structural awareness**: when you see the pattern in the chart, you stop fighting about the surface and start translating across the gap. Couples who learn this vocabulary together do markedly better.

We followed up six months later. They're engaged. The chart helped, in their words, "more than two years of therapy."

## Couple #2 — The "Zero Element" Imbalance

**Anonymized Profile:** *Married 11 years, two kids, persistent low-grade marital fatigue. She has 0 Water in her 8 characters. He has 0 Fire.*

This pair has the quieter, harder pattern — not active conflict but slow desynchronization.

- **0 Water** in her chart = low natural reservoir for emotional flexibility, recovery, intuition. She runs on willpower and structure.
- **0 Fire** in his chart = low natural baseline for excitement, expressiveness, social warmth. He runs on calm and depth.

Without the missing elements, neither partner can supply what the other partner can't generate. She wants more emotional warmth; he literally has no Fire to give. He wants more emotional flow; she literally has no Water to provide.

The Saju framing reframes the resentment. "He's not withholding warmth — he doesn't have a Fire reserve. I'm not cold — I don't have a Water reserve. Both of us need to source these elements externally."

In their case: she added morning ocean walks (Water), he picked up amateur singing in a community group (Fire). Three months later, the marriage stabilized. Not from working on each other. From each filling their own elemental gap.

## Couple #3 — The "Mismatched Daewoon" Pair

**Anonymized Profile:** *Dating 18 months, both early 30s, both report "great connection but feels off-rhythm."*

This pair has compatible Day Masters and good Ten Gods overlap. The compatibility module rated them **8/10 on chart compatibility**. So why did they feel off?

The Daewoon (10-year luck cycles) explained it.

- **He's in his Wealth decade** (재성대운) — career-building, accumulation, focused on professional consolidation
- **She's in her Output decade** (식상대운) — creative expression, exploration, identity-discovery

Both decades are strong individually. But they pull at different rhythms. He wants to build and stabilize. She wants to explore and express. Neither is wrong; the macro tide just isn't in phase.

The Saju recommendation here is the most subtle: **don't break up over a phase mismatch**. The decades cycle. In four years his Wealth decade ends and her Output decade ends; their next decades line up much better.

They decided to stay, knowing the rhythm gap is real but temporary. That's exactly what the Daewoon layer is for — naming what's structural vs what's circumstantial.

## The Five Recurring Patterns

Across the thousands of readings, five compatibility patterns repeat:

1. **Officer × Output (rules vs. defiance)** — most common conflict pattern
2. **Element-imbalance × Element-imbalance** — slow desync, both running on depleted reservoirs
3. **Daewoon mismatch** — chart-compatible but rhythm-divergent
4. **Same Day Master collision** — too much sameness, no contrast
5. **Wealth × Resource imbalance** — one partner's drive depletes the other's reservoir

Each pattern has a recommended language for the couple to use. Once you have the vocabulary, the conflict stops feeling personal — it starts feeling structural. That alone resolves a lot of it.
