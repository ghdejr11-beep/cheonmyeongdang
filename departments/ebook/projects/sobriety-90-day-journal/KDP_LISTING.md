## Description (4000 char max)

**Ninety days. One small page a day. The recovery companion that opens to help you in the hardest moments.**

If you're 1 day, 30 days, or 6 months into your decision to quit, this 90-day journal walks beside you. Three to five minutes a day. No willpower lectures. No 12-step doctrine forced on you. Just a structured place to put down what you're carrying so it stops carrying you.

**INSIDE EACH DAILY PAGE**
- Mood, sleep, and craving intensity (0-10) in 10 seconds
- Today's intention - one line that points the day
- Three gratitudes - the practice that rewires the brain
- Triggers and urges today
- What I did instead of using
- Who I reached out to + meeting attended
- One small win
- Evening reflection space

**WEEKLY CHECK-INS** surface patterns - which days were hardest, what helped, what to change next week. This is how you turn 90 days into data instead of a blur.

**MILESTONE REFLECTIONS** at Day 30, Day 60, and Day 90 capture the real transformation - the shifts you'll forget if you don't write them down.

**EMERGENCY PLAN** in the back - 7 evidence-based steps to ride out a craving without using. Read it out loud when you need to. Includes SAMHSA's 24/7 helpline (1-800-662-HELP), AA, SMART Recovery, and 988 Crisis Line.

**SLIP LOG (NO SHAME)** - because relapse is data, not a verdict. A place to learn from what happened so it doesn't repeat.

**SUPPORT TEAM DIRECTORY** - your people, your numbers, in one place you can find at 2am.

**WHO THIS IS FOR**
- Anyone choosing recovery from alcohol or drugs
- People in AA, SMART Recovery, or going it alone
- The "California Sober" or "Sober Curious" exploring 90 days
- Friends and family supporting someone in early recovery (gift)

**FORMAT**
- 6x9 inches - fits in a bag, slips into a backpack
- 114 pages, matte cover, print-on-demand
- Designed for daily use, not display

**A NOTE ON SAFETY**
This is a self-help journal, not medical advice. If you are physically dependent on alcohol or benzodiazepines, withdrawal can be life-threatening. Talk to a doctor or call SAMHSA before you stop drinking abruptly.
