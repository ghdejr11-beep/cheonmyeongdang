## Answer (314 words)

**Sinsal (신살, 神煞)** are named patterns or "special stars" in Korean Saju — specific stem-branch combinations or positional patterns that carry pre-defined symbolic meaning. They're one of the layers Korean Saju keeps richer than modern simplified Bazi.

**What they are**

When a Saju master examines your 8 characters, they're looking for not just element distribution and Ten Gods roles, but also for **named patterns** that have accumulated symbolic interpretations over centuries. These are the Sinsal.

**Examples of common Sinsal**

- **Cheoneul Gwiin (천을귀인, "Noble Helper")** — the most auspicious Sinsal. Indicates that helpful people show up at critical moments. Found in charts that have a specific stem-branch combination.
- **Dohwa (도화, "Peach Blossom")** — charisma and aesthetic attractiveness, sometimes including romantic complications. Common in charts of public figures.
- **Yeokma (역마, "Travel Horse")** — movement, travel, and change as a life theme. Common in charts of people who relocate often or work in mobile professions.
- **Baekho (백호, "White Tiger")** — sudden change patterns, ancestral karma flavor.
- **Gongmang (공망, "Empty Place")** — areas of life where things "fall through" or feel insubstantial.
- **Hwagae (화개, "Dharma Flower")** — spirituality, scholarship, religious or artistic depth.

**How they're used**

Sinsal don't override the main read — they *flavor* it. A chart with Cheoneul Gwiin reads differently than the same chart without. A chart with Dohwa reads differently than the same chart without.

**Why Korean Saju keeps more Sinsal than modern Bazi**

Korean Saju preserved a richer Sinsal library through 700 years of unbroken practice. Modern simplified Bazi (especially in mainland China after the Cultural Revolution disruption) often skips most of these for a more streamlined read.
