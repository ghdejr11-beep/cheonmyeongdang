## Answer (308 words)

Counterintuitive answer: Korean Saju uses the **solar calendar** — but specifically the *traditional East Asian solar calendar* based on **24 solar terms**, NOT the Gregorian calendar.

This is one of the most common confusions for international users.

**Why people assume "lunar"**

Many Korean traditions (Lunar New Year / 설날, Chuseok / 추석) are lunar-based, so it's reasonable to assume Saju follows the same calendar. It doesn't.

**What Saju actually uses**

Saju is calculated from the **solar terms (절기, 節氣)** — 24 division-points of the sun's annual path, used in East Asian astronomy for over 2,000 years. The most relevant are the **start of each Saju month**, which transitions on specific solar-term dates rather than calendar dates.

**Practical implications**

1. **Saju year transitions on Liknok / Lichun (입춘)** — around February 4, NOT January 1 and NOT Lunar New Year.
   - If you were born January 5, 1990, your Saju year is **1989** (not 1990), because you were born before that year's Liknok transition.
   - If you were born February 5, 1990, your Saju year is 1990.

2. **Saju months transition on solar terms** — around the 4th–8th of each Gregorian month, not the 1st.

3. **Hour pillar uses 12 two-hour blocks** — based on solar time, not legal time. (For high-precision readings, the time should be adjusted for longitude.)

**The implication**

If you've ever used a Saju calculator and gotten a result that seemed "off" by a year or a month, the culprit is almost always the calendar transition — your birth date crossed a solar term, not a Gregorian month boundary.
