## Answer (312 words)

**Saju (사주)** literally means "Four Pillars" in Korean, and it is essentially the Korean evolution of **Bazi (八字, "Eight Characters")** — the same family tree, but with different cultural emphasis.

**Where they came from**

Both systems originated in ancient China (Tang dynasty refinements, ~7th–10th century). Korea adopted the framework around the Goryeo–Joseon era and developed it for ~700 years inside Korean society. Today the two cousins read the same chart but with notably different priorities.

**Same core mechanics**

- **Eight characters** = Year + Month + Day + Hour, each with one Heavenly Stem (天干) + one Earthly Branch (地支)
- **Five Elements (오행/五行)**: Wood, Fire, Earth, Metal, Water
- **Ten Gods (십성/十神)**: relational interpretations like Direct Officer, Hurting Officer, Wealth, Resource, etc.
- **Luck cycles (대운/大運)**: 10-year periods that flow over the natal chart

**Where Korean Saju diverges**

1. **Heavier weight on relationships and family roles.** Korean Confucian culture meant Saju developed deep tools for marriage compatibility (궁합), in-law harmony, and ancestral karma — areas often lighter in modern Bazi practice.
2. **Sinsal (신살, special stars).** Korean Saju keeps a much wider library of "special stars" — Cheoneul Gwiin (천을귀인, Noble Helper), Dohwa (도화, Peach Blossom for charisma), Yeokma (역마, Travel Horse), Baekho (백호, White Tiger). Modern simplified Bazi schools often skip most of these.
3. **Daewoon-driven life narrative.** Koreans tend to read Saju as a **life-arc story** ("the 30s are your Wealth decade, the 40s are your Officer decade") more than a static personality readout.
4. **Practitioner culture.** In Korea, Saju is mainstream — visited at weddings, before exams, at New Year. In Mainland China, official Bazi practice was disrupted during the Cultural Revolution, so the living tradition is stronger in Taiwan, Hong Kong, and Korea.
