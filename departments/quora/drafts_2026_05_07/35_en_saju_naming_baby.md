## Answer (322 words)

Yes — and the practice is much more common than international audiences usually realize. Naming consultants who specialize in Saju-aligned names are a real and active profession in Korea, and even modern, secular Korean families often consult Saju when choosing newborn names.

**How it works**

When a baby is born, the parents (or a Saju consultant) generate the baby's 8-character chart from the exact birth date, time, and place. The chart reveals:

- The baby's **Day Master** (core archetype)
- The baby's **Five-Element distribution** (what they have abundance of, what they have zero of)
- The baby's **dominant Ten Gods** (natural roles)
- The baby's **strength assessment** (whether the Day Master is structurally strong or weak)

From this analysis, the consultant identifies the baby's **favorable elements (용신)** — the elements the chart needs to balance out gaps or moderate excesses.

**The naming step**

In Korean, names are typically two characters (sometimes one or three). Each Hanja (Chinese character) used in Korean names has its own elemental association — Wood-coded characters, Fire-coded characters, Earth-coded characters, etc.

The consultant chooses Hanja whose elements **fill the chart's gaps**. A chart missing Water might receive a name with Water-coded characters; a chart with weak Day Master might receive a name with elements that strengthen the Day Master.

**Why it matters culturally**

In Korean tradition, the name is treated as a *sustained input* into the person's life — pronounced thousands of times, written, signed, used as identity. The Saju logic is that this sustained input can compensate for natal chart gaps.

**Modern usage**

Even Korean families that are otherwise secular often consult a Saju naming specialist for newborns. The cost is typically modest, and the practice is socially normal — comparable to consulting a baby-name book in the West, but with structural reasoning.
